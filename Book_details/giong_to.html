Giong To

"Giong to" is a tragedy depicting the lowliness and lack of integrity of human beings in all aspects of life: no one can trust anyone, no one can rely on anyone. From within to outside, from siblings to parents, from wives to husbands, from fathers to children, all live in deceit, selfishness, a closed loop of incest: crime and deception are rampant everywhere, and the consequences are unpredictable.

"Giong to" is not a novel like "Tat den" by Ngo Tat To or "Doan tuyet" by Nhat Linh. It is not just about exposing the corruption in the feudal village system, the exploitation of the rich and officials in the countryside during the French colonial period, like Ngo Tat To. It does not dismantle the traditional family system of the early 20th century society, like Nhat Linh.

Vu Trong Phung portrays human beings in the most realistic way in all times: the constant change of heart of human beings in a society where money can dominate everything.

While other authors only depict the victims of the system, such as Loan in "Doan tuyet," the victim of the mother-in-law and daughter-in-law system; Dau in "Tat den," the victim of heavy taxes and corrupt officials; Binh in "Bi vo," the victim of betrayal by a lover, the cruelty of parents, and the harshness of society, etc.

The characters in Vu Trong Phung's works are different: in "Giong to," we cannot find any faces that are overly sympathetic or overly detestable, even Nghi Hach and Thi Mich, who are two contrasting characters, the rapist and the victim.

In "Giong to" (as well as in "Vo de" and "So do"), there is no clear division between victims and perpetrators, so critics like Truong Chinh, who are accustomed to dividing things into good and bad, cannot understand the complexity of Thi Mich's character.
