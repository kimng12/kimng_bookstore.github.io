Romeo and Juliet

Romeo and Juliet is a tragedy written by William Shakespeare in the late 16th century. It tells the story of two young lovers from feuding families in Verona, Italy. Romeo, a member of the Montague family, and Juliet, a member of the Capulet family, fall deeply in love despite their families' bitter rivalry.

The play explores themes of love, fate, and violence, as well as the impact of societal expectations on individuals. It features several memorable characters, including Romeo's best friend Mercutio, the hot-headed Tybalt, and the well-intentioned but misguided Friar Laurence.

The play's most famous lines include Romeo's declaration of love, "But soft, what light through yonder window breaks? It is the east, and Juliet is the sun," and Juliet's lament, "What's in a name? That which we call a rose / By any other word would smell as sweet."

Despite their efforts to be together, Romeo and Juliet's love ultimately leads to tragedy, as they are unable to overcome the barriers of their families' feud. The play has been adapted countless times in various forms, including film, television, and stage productions, and remains one of Shakespeare's most enduring works.
